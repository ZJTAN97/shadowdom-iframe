Executive Summary

An Article from Straits Times. China and Russia are "friends forever, never enemies," Chinese's Foreign Minister Wang Yi said in remarks published on April 1 during a visit to Moscow in which he also welcomed signs of normalising ties between Washington and Moscow. "The principle of 'friends forever, never enemies' ... serves as a solid legal basis for advancing strategic cooperation at a higher level," Mr Wang told Russia's RIA state news agency in an interview while Wang is on a three-day visit to Moscow for strategic cooperation talks, a trip overshadowed by uncertainty around talks to secure a ceasefire in Ukraine and US President Donald Trump's criticism of the Russian and Ukrainian leaders.

Trade gravity has shifted east

Customs data referenced earlier in this article shows China overtaking Germany and the United States as Russia's leading import partner, with finished vehicle exports to Russia reaching 287,000 units — nearly double the next destination. The trend underwrites the "no-limits" framing of Mr Wang's Moscow visit and helps explain why sanctions have not dislodged the partnership.

Energy flows tell a similar story. Russian seaborne crude exports to China have climbed sharply since early 2022, with discounted barrels routed through Pacific ports replacing volumes that previously cleared Rotterdam and Hamburg. Yuan-denominated settlement, once a marginal experiment, now covers a majority share of the bilateral commodity trade — a development the Kremlin frames as evidence of a durable post-dollar corridor.

For Beijing the calculus is more pragmatic than ideological. Cheap energy and a captive market for Chinese manufactured goods support the domestic growth target without exposing state banks to direct sanctions risk, provided transactions stay clear of dual-use categories. Analysts note that the most politically sensitive trade lines — semiconductors, machine tools with military applications — remain tightly throttled.

  • 23.7% — China's share of Russian imports in 2020, up from low single digits at the start of the century.
  • 287,000 finished vehicles shipped from China to Russia in the first five months of 2023.
  • 40+ — number of Xi–Putin meetings over the past decade, per Chinese state media.

Diplomatic ambiguity remains

Despite the warm rhetoric, Beijing has so far declined to provide direct material support for Russia's war effort, mindful of secondary sanctions and its trade exposure to Europe. Mr Wang's call to "eradicate the causes of the crisis through dialogue" signals continued positioning as a mediator rather than a co-belligerent.

That posture is reinforced by the joint China–Brazil framework circulated last year, which floated general principles for ending the conflict without naming an aggressor or proposing territorial terms. Western capitals received the document tepidly; Moscow welcomed its non-prescriptive tone. The drafting choice — broad enough to be acceptable, narrow enough to be unactionable — is consistent with Beijing's broader pattern of agenda-setting through procedural neutrality.

Wang's framing in the RIA interview hints at how Beijing wants the next phase read: peace as a process, not a settlement; major powers as stabilisers rather than guarantors; and bilateral coordination with Moscow as a precondition for any durable outcome. None of those formulations commits China to specific concessions, but each constrains how Washington and Brussels can characterise the partnership going forward.

  • No public commitment of lethal aid; dual-use export controls reportedly tightened on select categories.
  • Continued advocacy for a negotiated track, in coordination with Brazil and select Global South partners.
